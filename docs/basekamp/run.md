# How to Run a Basekamp


In the previous chapter you learned how to build a Basekamp. In this chapter you will learn how to run it. How to get people there, how to organise the work, what kind of system to setup and how to prevent issues. 


## Why bring people

There are a few reasons why its important to bring in people
1. There is a lot of work to be done. Specially in the early days. Infrastructure to setup, houses to build and land to regenerate. With more people you can get more stuff done!
2. The long term goal is to build a community. For this you need people, buts its very hard to select on this criteria. A good way is to organically roll into it. Which means some of the pioneering people that come helping in the early days might actually become long term members of your project. 

:::tip
The early days in Basekamp are raw. Infrastructure is minimal and basic. Finding the right people that like early stages is important.
:::


## How to intergrate people 

### Where to find 

**People you know**
There are several ways to find people to come and join. The safest way is probably friends and family or other people you already know. This makes it much easier to know upfront what to expect and building trust between each other is likely much easier. 

<img src="/assets/start/map.png"/>

**The map!**
Another great way is using our map in our online community. We specifically developed a tool that helps you to find people around you. Browser around and find other people that 'want to get started'. Or add yourself to the map so people can find and reach out to you. The map is a great way to find likeminded people. 
Friends family, map online

**Other Platforms**
Besides these two methods you can also use other existing platforms like Woofing, wWrkaway or post messages in forums or social media. That said this method is a bit harder as your trhow yourself out there in the world wide web. It can be hard to see properly who is on the other side and wether you are aligned in your mission. 


### How to invite 
You probably have a wide range of tasks that need to be done. From plumbing to cooking to wiring electricity. Its useful to put these applications online publicly so people can see what needs to be done and wether it fits their skills. You can use tools online ot list them on your own website. It pays of to describe the role you're looking for properly, with the dates people can come, expectation you have etc. To avoid wasting time from both sides by not having a good match.

An effective way to get the information you need from people is to have them fill in a form. Ask questions you need to udnerstand more about them. **Here you can find an example of our questions.** Once you get an interesting application, have them drop by or hop on a call to see if its match. Clarify the skills they have, explain about your setting and make sure to share the ups and downsides to sketch a realistic example. 

::tip
Screening people can be a difficult thing, but your quickly learn and improve. It will get better :)
:::



### How to intergrate onsite

Once You find the right people its time to onboard and intergrate them in your project!
This can be quite a tiring process if you dont have a good structure for it. Here a few things we recommend


1. Send important information upfront: Its useful to send people some useful information upfront before they arrive so they can prepare back home. Practical things like how to get there, classic things people forget(rain gear or biodegradable soaps)
Besides this we also send a disclaimer stating people come at their own risk and will be on camera. All of this is good to send upfront as people still have a change to opt-out (actually never happened in our case)
2. Calender: This is useful to keep the overview of who comes when and until what time they stay. Specially if many people come and go its easy to loose track of this
3. Arrival: Once people arrive there is a lot to jump into. Its nice to minimize work in the first week to let people land and introduce them properly. Here a few things part of the introduction. 
- Give a tour of the land
- Show them Basekamp (shower, toilet, waste system etc) 
- Setup their place to sleep 
- Introduce them to all our structures and guidelines.
- Safety instructions, first aid box, emergency contacts etc.

:::tip
Bonus: Setup a sytem that is easy for people to onboard eachother. Many people find it fun to onboard new members and it saves a lot of work on the people that are their longer to not having to onboard everyone. 
:::




## How to organise work

<img src="/assets/run/talk.jpg"/>

Getting work done is a key thing for Basekamp. There is lots of work to do in many areas. In the beginning you might be able to handle this with one small team. But as the project grows its good to distribute the responsibility of areas. It helps to organise in different groups. This allows people to build teams, develop specific skills, go deeper in their knowledge and hold responsibility of certain areas.

We call these areas, Pods. A few of the basic Pods that many will have are. Kitchen, Workspace, Basekamp and Landscape. But each project has a different missions and task. For instance we also have a video Pod as we upload weekly to Youtube. Others might have a Gardening or Animal Pod.

This is what the basics Pods do in our setting.

### Workspace Pod

The workspace develops projects and infrastructure. From electricity and water systems to building structures. It includes a shared workspace equipped with tools for working with metal, wood, and other materials. Since the indoor space is relatively small, most tools are portable and work often happens outside.

A key principle is that at the end of each day, all tools are cleaned and returned to their proper place. Because this operates in a community setting, organization is essential. It ensures shared responsibility, makes it easy to see what’s missing, and keeps the workspace functional and safe for everyone.

How the Workspace Operates
- Briefs: Each project usually begins with a clear brief. Upfront alignment happens on Budget. timeline, people involved
- Multiple projects: Separate teams work on different workspace projects simultaneously. On a weekly basis, the entire workspace pod meets to review progress, coordinate efforts, and support one another.
-  Maintenance & Purchasing Board: A board lists items that need to be bought, repaired, or maintained. One designated person oversees this board and is responsible for tools and safety.
- Project Milestone Calendar: A shared calendar tracks milestones and provides clarity on the status of each project.
- Dedicated Purchasing Role: One person handles purchasing materials, speaks the local language, and understands quality standards and sourcing practices.
- Material Standardization: Materials such as screws, wood, cables, and pipes are standardized to encourage reuse, reduce waste, and simplify repairs.
- Local Supplier Network: A trusted network of local suppliers, built over time, provides the materials needed for projects.


### Kitchen Pod

The Kitchen Pod operates from a simple but effective setup. Food is stored in fridges for fresh products and in large containers for dry goods. Lunch and dinner are prepared daily on fixed times. (in our case at 13:00 and 19:00.) When the meal is ready, a horn is sounded to call people to eat.

After eating, everyone washes their own basin outside. We use a Basin system to clean. Rough wash, clean with soap, rinse with water. Then dry it and return it to its place.

In addition to personal dishes, cooking equipment is cleaned every day. To distribute this workload fairly, cleaning teams are organized (further details provided separately).

How the Kitchen Pod Operates

- Buying: Shopping happens twice a week.
- Suppliers: Vegetables are sourced from a local farm. Bulk food is purchased from a local store.
- Storage: Fresh food is stored in fridges and organized with labels to maintain clarity and order. Dry goods are stored in large containers, also labeled with useful information such as cooking times.

In the Downloadkit you can find the labels we use the organise our containers.

### Landscape Pod

The landscape Pod carries out landscaping work, including clearing land, shaping terrain with a digger, ongoing maintenance, and planting trees. All tools are stored in a dedicated container. At the end of each day, all tools are returned to their place. Tools are used by many, organization is essential.

The work is divided into two main categories:
1. Maintenance: Ongoing, recurring work that happens throughout the year. This includes general upkeep and care of the land.
2. Projects: Larger regeneration efforts that require more time, planning, and coordination. These projects focus on shaping and improving the landscape in more structural ways.

How the Landscape Pod operates
- Labeling system: When a tool has an issue, a label is attached. This makes it easy to assess whether maintenance, repair, or sharpening is needed.
- Map: A map with zones of the landscape helps provide clarity on maintenance areas and project areas.
- Meetings: The team meets weekly for a check-in, coordination, and to organize the upcoming work.

### Basekamp Pod

The Basekamp Pod oversees the area where the community lives and works. Basekamp. Its role is to ensure people stay happy and everything functions smoothly, focusing on both the environment and infrastructure. 

What this Pod does:

- Vegetation Maintenance: Managing bushes, fallen trees, and clearing areas for use. Grass is chopped regularly to keep the area safe and accessible.
- Infrastructure Upkeep: Maintaining toilets, showers, compost systems, water filters, and waste systems. Ensuring cleanliness and functionality.
- Safety & Emergency: Checking fire extinguishers, first aid kits, and informing people about safety plans.
- Community Coordination: Supporting people who arrive on site, addressing issues, and being a point of contact for neighbors.





## How to organise people


Basekamp helps people work together on long-term projects while enjoying the process. With many things to build and land to regenerate, clear systems keep the space productive, safe, and enjoyable.


### Guidelines 
Everyone comes from different backgrounds. Its useful to have shared values and guidelines clearly written. It gives people orientation and helps to align on expectations. Examples of guidelines: Vegan meals during the week, Project car is not used for private rides, no loud noises in the weekend.

### Cleaning & Maintenance 
Everyone has different habits and stays for different periods. Clear systems ensure smooth operations.

**Weekly Overview:**
- Monday meetings align everyone and update the week’s plan.
- A shared board tracks schedules, arrivals, departures, events, and cleanup responsibilities.

**Cleanup Teams:**
- Each person is part of a team.
- Daily tasks include setting up breakfast, cleaning kitchens, tidying shared spaces, emptying trash and toilets.
- Checklists make it easy for everyone to join and know what to do.

**Friday Maintenance:**
- Weekly deep-clean and repair session (2 hours).
- Tasks include fixing chairs, stairs, or cleaning worn areas.
- Everyone picks a task, keeps the space functional, and creates a sense of accomplishment.

<img src="/assets/run/board.png"/>

### Life/Work Balance 
Balance between work and leisure is essential to maintain energy and happiness.

**Community & Social Time:**
- Human Hour: weekly meeting for non-practical sharing and community building.
- Evening events: pizza night, movie night, crafting, freestyle presentations.

**Workweek Structure:**
- Work happens during the week; weekends are off.
- Work times are team-based: workspace early, kitchen later.
- Resting is encouraged, even for passionate participants.
- Some guidelines apply to weekends (e.g., no power tools).

All structures may sound complex, but in practice, they keep Basekamp tidy, functional, and enjoyable. Systems are reviewed yearly and adjusted if needed to keep the balance between structure and flexibility.

## Troubleshooting

Here a few common topics you will probably bump into. Read them to get prepared or find some tips on how to deal with it

**1. Manage Intensity:** Being in a Basekamp can be intense. All infrastructure is in one place, and people are around all the time. People arrive with different energy levels: newcomers often bring lots of excitement, while longer-term residents may prefer a calmer pace. Creating dedicated quiet areas or outdoor spaces like yoga decks and accessible lagoons can help balance the energy.

**2. Personal Projects:** Many people arrive eager to start personal projects, such as building chairs, firepits, gardening, or making kombucha. These projects are fun and exciting, but they can take up space, increase maintenance, or stay longer then the person that started it. Having guidelines for starting personal projects helps keep the space functional and avoids conflicts.

**3. Communication:** Communication is key. Things change daily, and announcements need to flow quickly. Weekly meetings help, but a fast internal chat channel like WhatsApp, Telegram, or Signal keeps everyone informed in real time.

**4. Accept Imperfection:**  This can be difficult. If you want to live more sustainably there are always things to improve. But some things are really important for some people as it brings them comfort. For instance, coffee might not be the best drink, but very essential for some. Or eating vegan doesnt work for everyone, or some people really need a long hot shower to recharge. It will be a balance to find out what things you can change and what is to important to improve. 

**5. Harsh Conditions:** Harsh climate and basic infrastructure can make some seasons more challenging. In the early stages, working seasonally rather than year-round can reduce strain and also allow time to recharge, reflect, and plan next steps.

**6. Visitors:** Visitors are nice, but constant comings and goings can be overwhelming. Limiting visits to certain days, such as Fridays and weekends, and checking in with the group helps manage this.

**7. Minimum Stay:**  People staying longer helps them to understand the place and its systems better. A minimum stay of three months is recommended, as it reduces workload and maintains continuity.

:::tip
Early days bring constant challenges, so it’s important to stay open, learn, and adapt. If more help is needed, the website provides a space to post questions, and the community can offer support.
:::
